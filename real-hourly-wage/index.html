The idea

The concept comes from Your Money or Your Life — the bestselling book by Vicki Robin and Joe Dominguez that reshaped how millions think about money.

Its core insight: money is something you trade your life energy for. Every dollar you earn cost you a slice of time you'll never get back. So the honest way to judge a purchase isn't "can I afford it?" — it's "is this worth the hours of my life it costs?"

Why your "hourly wage" is a lie

Say you earn $60,000 a year and work 40 hours a week. That looks like about $29/hour. But that number ignores almost everything:

  • Taxes — you never see a chunk of that salary.
  • Commute — unpaid time you only spend because of the job.
  • Prep — getting ready, decompressing, work that follows you home.
  • Job-related spending — the costs that exist only because you work.

Once you subtract the money and add back the time, the real number is often 30–50% lower than the wage on your contract.

The calculation

Real hourly wage = take-home pay ÷ all the hours work really costs you

Here's a worked example:

Annual take-home pay$52,000
Hours worked + commute + prep, per week~48 hrs
Annual life-hours given to work~2,496 hrs
Real hourly wage≈ $20.83 / hr

That $29/hour on paper? It's really closer to $21 — the true price of an hour of this person's life.

What changes when you see it

Once an expense is priced in hours, decisions get clearer — instantly and without willpower:

$12 lunch out~35 min of your life
$100 impulse buy~4.8 hours
$1,200 weekend trip~58 hours — a week and a half of work

Nothing here tells you to stop spending. It just makes the trade honest. Some things are absolutely worth the hours. Many quietly aren't — and you only notice once you can see it.
